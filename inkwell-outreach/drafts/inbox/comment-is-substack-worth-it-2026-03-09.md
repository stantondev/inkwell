Appreciate the honest assessment here. I think the answer to "is it worth it" depends a lot on what you're measuring. If the question is "can I build an audience and earn money writing," then yes, Substack can still work for that, especially if you have the patience for it.

But there's a different version of that question that I think a lot of writers are really asking: "Can I trust this platform with my work and my audience long-term?" And that's harder to answer for any platform you don't control.

The 2-6% paid conversion stat you mentioned is telling. That means for most writers, the overwhelming majority of their audience is free subscribers who found them through the platform's distribution. If the platform changes how distribution works (and it just did, in January), those writers don't lose subscribers on paper. They just quietly stop being seen. That's the part that keeps me up at night.

I'm a PM who builds products, and that exact anxiety is what pushed me to start Inkwell (inkwell.social). It's a social journaling platform where there's no algorithm at all. Chronological feed, your followers see your work because they chose to, and it's connected to the fediverse so your audience isn't locked inside one platform's walls.

It's small. About 40 writers, public beta, built by one person (me). That's either a dealbreaker or a selling point depending on what you're looking for. What I can tell you is that in a community that size, people actually read each other's writing. Nobody is shouting into a void or trying to game a recommendation engine.

If the "is it worth it" question has been nagging at you, it might be worth seeing what a different model looks like. Not as a replacement for what's working, but as a space that's built differently from the ground up.
