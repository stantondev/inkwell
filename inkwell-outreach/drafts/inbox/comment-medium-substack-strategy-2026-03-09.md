This is a well-written breakdown, and I think the advice is solid for anyone committed to making Substack work. But I keep coming back to something that nags at me every time I read one of these strategy guides: writers shouldn't need a distribution strategy just to be read by the people who already follow them.

The fact that there's a whole genre of "how to beat the algorithm" posts for writing platforms tells us something. It means the platform's goals and the writer's goals have quietly diverged. You're not writing for your readers anymore. You're writing for a system that decides whether your readers get to see your work.

I hit this wall myself. I'm a product manager, I've spent years watching platforms follow the same arc. Open up, attract creators, then slowly tighten the funnel until reach becomes something you have to earn back. It's not malicious. It's just how ad-driven and growth-stage platforms work. Their incentives eventually point somewhere other than "show this person's writing to the people who asked to see it."

That frustration is why I started building Inkwell (inkwell.social). It's a social journaling platform, no algorithm, chronological feed, connected to the fediverse so your followers can find you from Mastodon and other platforms. It's genuinely small right now (about 40 writers in public beta), and I'm building it solo. But the smallness is actually part of what works. People read each other's entries. There's no strategy required beyond writing something you care about and hitting publish.

I'm not saying anyone should abandon what's working for them. But if you've ever felt like you're spending more time optimizing for a platform than actually writing, it might be worth checking out a different kind of space. The community votes on what gets built next, so it stays oriented around what writers actually need: inkwell.social/roadmap
