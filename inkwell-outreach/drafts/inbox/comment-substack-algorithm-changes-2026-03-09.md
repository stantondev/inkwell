This is one of the clearest breakdowns I've seen of what happened in January. And I think you nailed the core dynamic: when a platform shifts toward prioritizing predictable engagement and larger editorial operations, independent writers lose visibility even if their work hasn't changed at all. It's not a quality problem. It's a structural one.

I've watched this pattern play out across platforms for years in my day job as a PM. The early stage is always generous. Generous reach, generous recommendations, generous growth. Then the platform matures, the incentives shift, and the creators who built their audience there realize the platform was lending them an audience, not giving them one.

That's not a knock on anyone using Substack. If it's working for you, keep going. But for me, the pattern got frustrating enough that I started building something different. Inkwell (inkwell.social) is a social journaling platform with no algorithm at all. Your feed is chronological. Your followers see your writing because they chose to follow you, and nothing sits between that choice and their feed.

It's early. Really early. About 40 writers in public beta, and I'm building it myself with AI-assisted development. But that smallness means something: people actually read each other's work. There's no strategy layer between you and your readers. It also connects to the fediverse, so your Mastodon followers can follow your Inkwell writing directly.

If you're curious what a writing platform looks like when the person building it is also one of the writers on it, take a look. The roadmap is public and the community votes on what gets built: inkwell.social/roadmap
